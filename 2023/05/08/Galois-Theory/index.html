# Symmetry

Definition. A linear transformation σ:R2→R2\sigma:\mathbb{R}^2\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^2σ:R2→R2 is called orthogonal if it is distance preserving; that is, if ∣U−V∣|U-V|∣U−V∣ denotes the distance between points UUU and VVV, then

∣σ(U)−σ(V)∣=∣U−V∣|\sigma(U)-\sigma(V)|=|U-V| ∣σ(U)−σ(V)∣=∣U−V∣

The set O(2,R)O(2,\mathbb{R})O(2,R) of all orthogonal transformations is a group under composition, called the real orthogonal group.

Definition.: Given a figure FFF in the plane, its symmetry group Σ(F)\Sigma(F)Σ(F) is the family of all orthogonal transformations σ:R2→R2\sigma:\mathbb{R}^2\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^2σ:R2→R2 for which

σ(F)=F\sigma(F)=F σ(F)=F

The elements of Σ(F)\Sigma(F)Σ(F) are called symmetries.

The wonderful idea of Galois was to associate to each polynomial f(x)f(x)f(x) a group, nowadays called its Galois group, whose properties reflect the behavior of f(x)f(x)f(x). Our aim in this section is to set up an analogy between the symmetry group of a polygon and the Galois group of a polynomial.

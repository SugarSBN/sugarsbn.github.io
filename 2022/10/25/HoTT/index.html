# Week 1

# HoTT in type theory context

  • ITT (Intensional type theory) is a intuitionistic type theory that serves as the core theory for other type theories. Other type theories are merely extensions of ITT .

  • ETT (Extensional type theory) extends ITT with ER (equality of reflection) and UIP (uniqueness of identity proofs):

    ETT=ITT+RT+UIPSETT=ITT+RT+UIPS ETT=ITT+RT+UIPS

    Since types are perceived as sets in ETT , ETT gives rise to a intuitionistic theory of sets.

  • HoTT extends ITT with HIT (higher inductive types) and UA (univalence axiom):

    HoTT=ITT+HIT+UAHoTT=ITT+HIT+UA HoTT=ITT+HIT+UA

    Since types are perceived as abstract spaces in HoTT , HoTT gives rise to a intuitionistic theory of weak  ∞−groupoidsweak\;\infty-groupoidsweak∞−groupoids.

# Intuitionistic propositional logic

Intuitionistic logic means it is a proof-relevant logic.

  • As advanced by Per Matin-Lof, a modern presentation of IPL (intuitionistic propositional logic) distinguishes the notions of judgment and proposition.

    A judgment is something that may be known, whereas a proposition is something that sensibly may be the subject of a judgment.

    In IPL , the two most basic judgements are A prop and A true:

    • A prop A is a well-formed proposition
    • A true Proposition A is intuitionistically true, i.e., has a proof.

  • The inference rules for the prop judgment are called formation rules.

    The inference rules for the true judgment are divided into classes: introduction rules and elimination rules.

# Entailment

The last form of proposition in the negative fragment of IPL is implication.
However, to define implication, a different form of judgment is required: entailment, which is written as:

A1  true,…,An  true⏟n≥0⊢A  true\begin{aligned} \underbrace{A_1\;true, \dots, A_n\;true}_{n \geq 0}\vdash A\;true \end{aligned} n≥0A1​true,…,An​true​​⊢Atrue​

we can understand the left part as the “context”, denoted by Γ\GammaΓ, or the assumptions for A  trueA\;trueAtrue. So we can rewrite the conjunction as:

Γ⊢A  trueΓ⊢B  trueΓ⊢A∧B  true(∧I)\begin{aligned} \frac{\Gamma\vdash A\;true\quad \Gamma\vdash B\;true}{\Gamma\vdash A\wedge B\;true}\quad(\wedge I) \end{aligned} Γ⊢A∧BtrueΓ⊢AtrueΓ⊢Btrue​(∧I)​

Reflexivity:

A  true⊢A  true(R)\frac{}{A\;true\vdash A\;true}\quad (R) Atrue⊢Atrue​(R)

Transitivity:

A  trueA  true⊢C  trueC  true(T)\frac{A\;true\quad A\;true\vdash C\;true}{C\;true}\quad (T) CtrueAtrueAtrue⊢Ctrue​(T)

Reflexivity and transitivity are undeniable properties of entailment
because they give meaning to assumptions—assumptions are strong enough to prove
conclusions (reflexivity), but are only as strong as the proofs they stand for (transitivity).

But there are also structural properties that can be denied: weakening ,
contraction , and permutation . Logics that deny any of these properties are called
substructural logics.

Weakening:

A  trueB  true⊢A  true(W)\frac{A\;true}{B\;true\vdash A\;true}\quad (W) Btrue⊢AtrueAtrue​(W)

Contraction:

A  true,A  true⊢C  trueA  true⊢C  true(C)\frac{A\;true,A\;true\vdash C\;true}{A\;true\vdash C\;true}\quad (C) Atrue⊢CtrueAtrue,Atrue⊢Ctrue​(C)

Denying contraction (along with weakening) leads to linear logic, in which we
wish to reason about the number of copies of an assumption. This is a powerful way
to express consumable resources. In this course, we will always have the principle of
contraction, however.

Permutation:

Γ⊢C  trueπ(Γ)⊢C  true(P)\frac{\Gamma\vdash C\;true}{\pi(\Gamma)\vdash C\;true}\quad (P) π(Γ)⊢CtrueΓ⊢Ctrue​(P)

where π(Γ)\pi(\Gamma)π(Γ) is a permutation of Γ\GammaΓ.

Denying permutation (along with weakening and contraction) leads to ordered,
or noncommutative, logic. It is a powerful way to express ordered structures, like
lists or even formal grammars. In this course, we will always have the principle of
permutation, however.

# Implication

A  propB  propA⊃B  prop(⊃F)\begin{aligned} \frac{A\;prop\quad B\;prop}{A\supset B\;prop}\quad (\supset F)\\ \end{aligned} A⊃BpropApropBprop​(⊃F)​

The introduction rule for implication is:

A  true⊢B  trueA⊃B  true(⊃I)\begin{aligned} \frac{A\;true\vdash B\;true}{A\supset B\;true}\quad (\supset I) \end{aligned} A⊃BtrueAtrue⊢Btrue​(⊃I)​

We use implication to internalize the entailment as a proposition: B  true⊢A  trueB\;true\vdash A\;trueBtrue⊢Atrue is not a proposition while B⊃AB\supset AB⊃A is a proposition.

We may write the elimination rule as A⊃B  trueA  true⊢B  true\frac{A\supset B\;true}{A\;true\vdash B\;true}Atrue⊢BtrueA⊃Btrue​. However, we can consider “A  true⊢B  trueA\;true\vdash B\;trueAtrue⊢Btrue” as some stuff like function. We would prefer the uncurried form of elimination rule, which is:

A⊃B  trueA  trueB  true(⊃E)\begin{aligned} \frac{A\supset B\;true\quad A\;true}{B\;true}\quad (\supset E) \end{aligned} BtrueA⊃BtrueAtrue​(⊃E)​

it’s somehow “(A, B) -> C” instread of “A -> (B -> C)”.

# Falsehood

The unit of disjunction is falsehood, the proposition that is trivially never true, which we write as ⊥\perp⊥. Its formation rule is immdeidate evidence that ⊥\perp⊥ is a well-formed proposition:

⊥  prop(⊥F)\begin{aligned} \frac{}{\perp\;prop}\quad (\perp F) \end{aligned} ⊥prop​(⊥F)​

Because ⊥\perp⊥ is never true, it has no introduction rule. However, it does have an elimination rule, which captures “ex falso quolibet”:

⊥  trueC  true(⊥E)\begin{aligned} \frac{\perp\;true}{C\;true}\quad (\perp E) \end{aligned} Ctrue⊥true​(⊥E)​

# Order-theoretic formulation of IPL

# Conjunction as meet

The elimination rules for conjunction (along with reflexivity of entailment) ensure
that A∧B  true⊢A  trueA\wedge B\;true\vdash A\;trueA∧Btrue⊢Atrue and A∧B  true⊢B  trueA\wedge B\;true\vdash B\;trueA∧Btrue⊢Btrue.
To ensure completeness of the order-theoretic formulation, we include the rules:

A∧B≤AA∧B≤B\begin{aligned} \frac{}{A\wedge B\leq A}\quad\frac{}{A\wedge B\leq B} \end{aligned} A∧B≤A​A∧B≤B​​

which say that A∧BA\wedge BA∧B is a lower bound of A and B.

The introduction rule for conjunction ensures that C  true⊢A∧B  trueC\;true\vdash A\wedge B\;trueCtrue⊢A∧Btrue if both
C  true⊢A  trueC\;true\vdash A\;trueCtrue⊢Atrue and C  true⊢B  trueC\;true\vdash B\;trueCtrue⊢Btrue. Order-theoretically, this is expressed as the rule:

C≤AC≤BC≤A∧B\frac{C\leq A\quad C\leq B}{C\leq A\wedge B} C≤A∧BC≤AC≤B​

which says that A∧BA\wedge BA∧B is larger than or equal to any lower bound of A and B.
Taken together these rules show that A∧BA\wedge BA∧B is the greatest lower bound, or meet, of A and B.

# Truth as greatest element

The introduction rule for truth ensures that C  true⊢⊤  trueC\;true\vdash \top\;trueCtrue⊢⊤true. Order-theoretically, we have

C≤⊤\frac{}{C\leq \top} C≤⊤​

which says that ⊤\top⊤ is the greatest, or final element.

# Disjunction as join

The introduction rules for disjunction ensure that A∨B  true⊢A  trueA\vee B\;true\vdash A\;trueA∨Btrue⊢Atrue and A∨B  true⊢B  trueA\vee B\;true\vdash B\;trueA∨Btrue⊢Btrue, we include the rules:

A≤A∨BB≤A∨B\frac{}{A\leq A\vee B}\quad\frac{}{B\leq A\vee B} A≤A∨B​B≤A∨B​

which say that A∨BA\vee BA∨B is an upper bound of A and B.

The elimination rule for disjunction (along with reflexivity of entailment) ensures that
A∨B  true⊢C  trueA\vee B\;true\vdash C\;trueA∨Btrue⊢Ctrue if both A  true⊢C  trueA\;true\vdash C\;trueAtrue⊢Ctrue and B  true⊢C  trueB\;true\vdash C\;trueBtrue⊢Ctrue.
Order-theorectically, we have the rule:

A≤CB≤CA∨B≤C\frac{A\leq C\quad B\leq C}{A\vee B\leq C} A∨B≤CA≤CB≤C​

which says that A∨BA\vee BA∨B is as small as any upper bound of A and B.
Taken together these rules show that A∨BA\vee BA∨B is the least upper bound, or join, of A and B.

# Falsehood as least element

The elimination rule for falsehood (along with reflexivity of entailment) ensures that
⊥  true⊢C  true\perp\;true\vdash C\;true⊥true⊢Ctrue. The order-theoretic counterpart is:

⊥≤C\frac{}{\perp\leq C} ⊥≤C​

which says that ⊥\perp⊥ is the least, or initial element.

# Implication as exponential

The elimination rule for implication (along with reflexivity of entailment) ensures that
A  true,A⊃B  true⊢BA\;true,A\supset B\;true\vdash BAtrue,A⊃Btrue⊢B. For the order-theoretic formulation to be complete, we include the rule:

A∧(A⊃B)≤B\frac{}{A\wedge(A\supset B)\leq B} A∧(A⊃B)≤B​

The introduction rule for implication ensures that C  true⊢A⊃B  trueC\;true\vdash A\supset B\;trueCtrue⊢A⊃Btrue if
A  true,C  true⊢B  trueA\;true,C\;true\vdash B\;trueAtrue,Ctrue⊢Btrue. We have:

A∧C≤BC≤A⊃B\frac{A\wedge C\leq B}{C\leq A\supset B} C≤A⊃BA∧C≤B​

Taken together, A⊃BA\supset BA⊃B is the exact upper bound of the set {X  ∣  A∧X≤B}\{X\;|\;A\wedge X\leq B\}{X∣A∧X≤B}.

What’s more, we can consider A⊃BA\supset BA⊃B as the exponential: BAB^ABA.
Why? We use a more familiar denotation “A→BA\rightarrow BA→B” for implication, you may realize that the set of functions from set A to set B is also denoted as BAB^ABA.
The process of currying and uncurrying is just the arithmetic law:

A→(B→C)≅(CB)A=CB∗A≅(A∧B)→C\begin{aligned} & A\rightarrow (B\rightarrow C)\cong (C^B)^A=C^{B*A}\cong (A\wedge B) \rightarrow C\\ \end{aligned} ​A→(B→C)≅(CB)A=CB∗A≅(A∧B)→C​

# Decidability and stability

Definition. A prop is decidable if and only if A∨¬A  trueA\vee\neg A\;trueA∨¬Atrue.

Decidability is what separates constructive logic from classic logic: in classic logic, every proposition is decidable while in constructive logic, it’s not.

  • ⊥,⊤\perp,\top⊥,⊤ are decidable.
  • We would expect m=Nnm=_\mathbb{N}nm=N​n is decidable, where =N=_\mathbb{N}=N​ denotes the quality of natural numbers.
  • we would not expect m=Rnm=_\mathbb{R}nm=R​n is decidable, where =R=_\mathbb{R}=R​ denotes the quality of real numbers, because they are infinite.

Definition. A prop is stable if and only if (¬¬A)⊃A  true(\neg\neg A)\supset A\;true(¬¬A)⊃Atrue.

Again, in classic logic, every proposition is stable, while in constructive logic let’s consider the following lemma:

Proof:
¬A=A⊃⊥\neg A=A\supset\perp¬A=A⊃⊥.

  • We first prove ¬(A∨¬A)  true⊢A⊃⊥  true\neg(A\vee\neg A)\;true\vdash A\supset\perp\;true¬(A∨¬A)true⊢A⊃⊥true:

A  trueA∨¬A  true(∨I1)¬(A∨¬A)  true⊥(⊃E)\begin{aligned} \cfrac{\cfrac{A\;true}{A\vee\neg A\;true}(\vee I_1)\quad \neg(A\vee\neg A)\;true}{\perp}(\supset E) \end{aligned} ⊥A∨¬AtrueAtrue​(∨I1​)¬(A∨¬A)true​(⊃E)​

    Then we have

# Disjunctive property

A theory T has the DP (disjunctive property) if T⊢A∨BT\vdash A\vee BT⊢A∨B implies T⊢AT\vdash AT⊢A or T⊢BT\vdash BT⊢B.

Theorem. In IPL , if ∅⊢A∨B  true\emptyset\vdash A\vee B\;true∅⊢A∨Btrue then ∅⊢A  true\emptyset\vdash A\;true∅⊢Atrue or ∅⊢B  true\emptyset\vdash B\;true∅⊢Btrue.

Naive attempt at a proof: The idea is to perform induction on all
possible derivations ∇\nabla∇ of ∅⊢A∨B  true\emptyset\vdash A\vee B\;true∅⊢A∨Btrue.
Since ∅⊢A∨B  true\emptyset\vdash A\vee B\;true∅⊢A∨Btrue can only be obtained by ∨I1,∨I2\vee I_1,\vee I_2∨I1​,∨I2​ and ⊃E,∧E,∨E,⊥E\supset E,\wedge E,\vee E,\perp E⊃E,∧E,∨E,⊥E.

If:

∇A  true∅⊢A∨B  true(∨I1)\cfrac{\cfrac{\nabla}{A\;true}}{\emptyset\vdash A\vee B\;true}(\vee I_1) ∅⊢A∨BtrueAtrue∇​​(∨I1​)

Here we already obtained a proof for A  trueA\;trueAtrue, ∨I2\vee I_2∨I2​ is just similar.

If:

∇1∅⊢C⊃A∨B  true∇2∅⊢C  true∅⊢A∨B  true(⊃E)\cfrac{\cfrac{\nabla_1}{\emptyset\vdash C\supset A\vee B\;true}\quad\cfrac{\nabla_2}{\emptyset \vdash C\;true}}{\emptyset\vdash A\vee B\;true}(\supset E) ∅⊢A∨Btrue∅⊢C⊃A∨Btrue∇1​​∅⊢Ctrue∇2​​​(⊃E)

# Admissible properties

Definition. A deduction rule is admissible in IPL if nothing changes when it is added to the existing rules of IPL .

Theorem. The structural properties of ⊢IPL\vdash_{IPL}⊢IPL​ are admissible.

Proof:
Firstly we can prove that Reflexivity, Contraction and Exchange are admissible. Take Reflexivity as an example:

Γ⊢A  trueΓ⊢A  true(R)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash A\;true}{\Gamma\vdash A\;true}(R) Γ⊢AtrueΓ⊢Atrue​(R)

it is admissible in that it can be substituted by:

Γ⊢A  trueΓ⊢A∧A  true(∧I)Γ⊢A  true(∧E)\cfrac{\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash A\;true}{\Gamma\vdash A\wedge A\;true}(\wedge I)}{\Gamma\vdash A\;true}(\wedge E) Γ⊢AtrueΓ⊢A∧AtrueΓ⊢Atrue​(∧I)​(∧E)

And for contraction, for any derivation ∇A  true,A  true⊢C  trueA  true⊢C  true\cfrac{\cfrac{\nabla}{A\;true,A\;true\vdash C\;true}}{A\;true\vdash C\;true}Atrue⊢CtrueAtrue,Atrue⊢Ctrue∇​​ we can substitute any A  true,A  trueA\;true,A\;trueAtrue,Atrue in ∇\nabla∇ by A  trueA\;trueAtrue.
So the rule does not change anything.

For weakening rule, we need to use the induction:

Suppose that Γ⊢B1  trueΓ,A  true⊢B1  true\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash B_1\;true}{\Gamma,A\;true\vdash B_1\;true}Γ,Atrue⊢B1​trueΓ⊢B1​true​ and Γ⊢B2  trueΓ,A  true⊢B2  true\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash B_2\;true}{\Gamma,A\;true\vdash B_2\;true}Γ,Atrue⊢B2​trueΓ⊢B2​true​ are admissible, then there will be derivation without weakening for them:

then we can prove that Γ⊢B1∧B2  trueΓ,A  true⊢B1∧B2  true\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash B_1\wedge B_2\;true}{\Gamma,A\;true\vdash B_1\wedge B_2\;true}Γ,Atrue⊢B1​∧B2​trueΓ⊢B1​∧B2​true​ is also admissible:

Γ⊢B1∧B2  trueΓ⊢B1  true(∧E1)∇1Γ,A  true⊢B1  trueΓ⊢B1∧B2  trueΓ⊢B2  true(∧E2)∇2Γ,A  true⊢B2  trueΓ,A  true⊢B1∧B2  true\cfrac{\cfrac{\cfrac{\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash B_1\wedge B_2\;true}{\Gamma\vdash B_1\;true}(\wedge E_1)}{\nabla_1}}{\Gamma,A\;true\vdash B_1\;true}\quad\cfrac{\cfrac{\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash B_1\wedge B_2\;true}{\Gamma\vdash B_2\;true}(\wedge E_2)}{\nabla_2}}{\Gamma,A\;true\vdash B_2\;true}}{\Gamma,A\;true\vdash B_1\wedge B_2\;true} Γ,Atrue⊢B1​∧B2​trueΓ,Atrue⊢B1​true∇1​Γ⊢B1​trueΓ⊢B1​∧B2​true​(∧E1​)​​Γ,Atrue⊢B2​true∇2​Γ⊢B2​trueΓ⊢B1​∧B2​true​(∧E2​)​​​

Then by induction we can shrink the derivation, until the proposition don’t have “∧,∨,¬\wedge,\vee,\neg∧,∨,¬”. The last proposition is proved by some rules other than weakening, so the weakening is admissible.

Other rules are proved in the same way. In a word, structural properties (Reflexivity, Contraction, Exchange, Weakening, Transitivity) are admissible, while negative and positive fragments (conjunction,disjunction)
are not.

We can think x1,...,xnx_1,...,x_nx1​,...,xn​ as the hypothesis for the proof,
but what we really want is for them to behave like varaibles, the proof
MMM will use the variables to prove.

# Structural Properties of Entailment with Proof Terms

  • Reflexivity/Variable rule:

Γ,x:A,Γ′⊢x:A(R/V)\cfrac{}{\Gamma,x:A,\Gamma'\vdash x:A}(R/V) Γ,x:A,Γ′⊢x:A​(R/V)

  • Transitivity/Substitution

Γ,x:A,Γ′⊢N:BΓ⊢M:AΓ,Γ′⊢[M/x]N:B(T/S)\cfrac{\Gamma,x:A,\Gamma'\vdash N:B\quad\Gamma\vdash M:A}{\Gamma,\Gamma'\vdash [M/x]N:B}(T/S) Γ,Γ′⊢[M/x]N:BΓ,x:A,Γ′⊢N:BΓ⊢M:A​(T/S)

which means, if the previous proof can prove the proposition AAA, then x:Ax:Ax:A is no longer needed. We can substitute xxx with MMM in the proof NNN of BBB.

  • Weakening:

Γ⊢M:AΓ,Γ′⊢M:A(W)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A}{\Gamma,\Gamma'\vdash M:A}(W) Γ,Γ′⊢M:AΓ⊢M:A​(W)

  • Contraction:
    If we have two different proofs for the same proposition x:A,y:Ax:A,y:Ax:A,y:A,then we can pick one of them z=xz=xz=x or z=yz=yz=y and substitute the other one with zzz:

Γ,x:A,y:A,Γ′⊢N:BΓ,z:A,Γ′⊢[z/x  or  z/y]N:B(C)\cfrac{\Gamma,x:A,y:A,\Gamma'\vdash N:B}{\Gamma,z:A,\Gamma'\vdash [z/x\;or\;z/y]N:B}(C) Γ,z:A,Γ′⊢[z/xorz/y]N:BΓ,x:A,y:A,Γ′⊢N:B​(C)

  • Exchange:

Γ,x:A,y:B,Γ′⊢N:CΓ,y:B,x:A,Γ′⊢N:C(X)\cfrac{\Gamma,x:A,y:B,\Gamma'\vdash N:C}{\Gamma,y:B,x:A,\Gamma'\vdash N:C}(X) Γ,y:B,x:A,Γ′⊢N:CΓ,x:A,y:B,Γ′⊢N:C​(X)

# Negative fraggment of IPL with proof terms

  • Truth: truth is trivially always true, so it doesn’t need any proof:

Γ⊢⟨  ⟩:⊤(⊤I)\cfrac{}{\Gamma\vdash\langle\;\rangle:\top}(\top I) Γ⊢⟨⟩:⊤​(⊤I)

  • Conjunction Introduction:We combine the proof M,NM,NM,N as ⟨M,N⟩\langle M,N\rangle⟨M,N⟩:

Γ⊢M:AΓ⊢N:BΓ⊢⟨M,N⟩:A∧B(∧I)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A\quad \Gamma\vdash N:B}{\Gamma\vdash \langle M,N\rangle:A\wedge B}(\wedge I) Γ⊢⟨M,N⟩:A∧BΓ⊢M:AΓ⊢N:B​(∧I)

  • Conjunction Elimination:

Γ⊢M:A∧BΓ⊢fst(M):A(∧E1)Γ⊢M:A∧BΓ⊢snd(M):B(∧E2)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A\wedge B}{\Gamma\vdash fst(M):A}(\wedge E_1) \quad \cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A\wedge B}{\Gamma\vdash snd(M):B}(\wedge E_2) Γ⊢fst(M):AΓ⊢M:A∧B​(∧E1​)Γ⊢snd(M):BΓ⊢M:A∧B​(∧E2​)

  • Implication Introduction: we may view the proof xxx as a variable:

Γ,x:A⊢M:BΓ⊢λx.M:A⊃B\cfrac{\Gamma,x:A\vdash M:B}{\Gamma\vdash \lambda x.M:A\supset B} Γ⊢λx.M:A⊃BΓ,x:A⊢M:B​

  • Implication Elimination:

Γ⊢M:A⊃BΓ⊢N:AΓ⊢M(N):B(⊃E)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A\supset B\quad\Gamma\vdash N:A}{\Gamma \vdash M(N):B}(\supset E) Γ⊢M(N):BΓ⊢M:A⊃BΓ⊢N:A​(⊃E)

# Definitional equality

We need the definitional equality to define the equality of two proofs:M:A,M′:AM:A,M':AM:A,M′:A are the same. We denote it as M≡M′:AM\equiv M':AM≡M′:A and want it to be the least congruence containing(closed under) the β\betaβ rules.

A congruence is an equivalence relation that respects our operations, for the equivalence relation that respects our operations, it basically means that if M≡M′:AM\equiv M':AM≡M′:A, then that their images under any operator should be equivalent. In
other words, we should be able to replace MMM with M′M'M′ everywhere. For example, it shoule be true that:

Γ⊢M≡M′:A∧BΓ⊢fst(M)≡fst(M′):A\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M\equiv M':A\wedge B}{\Gamma\vdash fst(M)\equiv fst(M'):A} Γ⊢fst(M)≡fst(M′):AΓ⊢M≡M′:A∧B​

There might be many congruences that contains the β\betaβ rules. Given two congruences ≡\equiv≡ and ≡′\equiv'≡′, we say
≡\equiv≡ is finer that ≡′\equiv'≡′ if M≡′M′:AM\equiv' M':AM≡′M′:A implies M≡M′:AM\equiv M':AM≡M′:A.

# Gentzen’s Inversion Principle

Gentzen’s Inversion Principle captures the intuition that “the elimination rules should cancel the introduction rules modulo definitional equility”.

The following are the β\betaβ rules for the negative fragment of IPL :

Conjunction:

Γ⊢M:AΓ⊢N:BΓ⊢fst(⟨M,N⟩)≡M:A∧B(β∧1)Γ⊢M:AΓ⊢N:BΓ⊢snd(⟨M,N⟩)≡N:A∧B(β∧2)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A\quad\Gamma\vdash N:B}{\Gamma\vdash fst(\lang M,N\rang)\equiv M:A\wedge B}(\beta\wedge_1)\\ \cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A\quad\Gamma\vdash N:B}{\Gamma\vdash snd(\lang M,N\rang)\equiv N:A\wedge B}(\beta\wedge_2) Γ⊢fst(⟨M,N⟩)≡M:A∧BΓ⊢M:AΓ⊢N:B​(β∧1​)Γ⊢snd(⟨M,N⟩)≡N:A∧BΓ⊢M:AΓ⊢N:B​(β∧2​)

Implication:

Γ,x:A⊢M:BΓ⊢N:AΓ⊢(λx.M)(N)≡[N/x]M:B(β⊃)\cfrac{\Gamma,x:A\vdash M:B\quad\Gamma\vdash N:A}{\Gamma\vdash (\lambda x.M)(N)\equiv[N/x]M:B}(\beta\supset) Γ⊢(λx.M)(N)≡[N/x]M:BΓ,x:A⊢M:BΓ⊢N:A​(β⊃)

# Gentzen’s Unicity Principle

Gentzen’s Unicity Principle captures the intuition that “there should be one way to prove something under definitional equivalence”.

Truth:

Γ⊢M:⊤Γ⊢M≡⟨  ⟩:⊤(η⊤)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:\top}{\Gamma\vdash M\equiv \lang\;\rang:\top}(\eta\top) Γ⊢M≡⟨⟩:⊤Γ⊢M:⊤​(η⊤)

Conjunction:

Γ⊢M:A∧BΓ⊢M≡⟨fst(M),snd(M)⟩:A∧B(η∧)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A\wedge B}{\Gamma\vdash M\equiv\lang fst(M),snd(M)\rang:A\wedge B}(\eta\wedge) Γ⊢M≡⟨fst(M),snd(M)⟩:A∧BΓ⊢M:A∧B​(η∧)

Implication:

Γ⊢M:A⊃BΓ⊢M≡λx.M(x):A⊃B(η⊃)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A\supset B}{\Gamma\vdash M\equiv\lambda x.M(x):A\supset B}(\eta\supset) Γ⊢M≡λx.M(x):A⊃BΓ⊢M:A⊃B​(η⊃)

# Categorical Theoretic Approach

In a Heyting Algebra, we have a preorder A≤BA\leq BA≤B when
AAA implies BBB.
However, we now wish to keep track of proofs, so if MMM

M:A→BM:A\rightarrow BM:A→B.

id:A→Aid:A\rightarrow A id:A→A

Composition:
We should be able to compose maps:

⟨  ⟩:A→1\langle\;\rangle:A\rightarrow 1 ⟨⟩:A→1

# Gentzen’s Inversion Principles (β\betaβ rules)

Recall the β\betaβ rules:

# Gentzen’s Unicity Principles (η\etaη rules)

Γ⊢M:A∧BΓ⊢M≡⟨fst(M),snd(M)⟩:A∧B(η∧)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A\wedge B}{\Gamma\vdash M\equiv\lang fst(M),snd(M)\rang:A\wedge B}(\eta\wedge) Γ⊢M≡⟨fst(M),snd(M)⟩:A∧BΓ⊢M:A∧B​(η∧)

Γ⊢M:A⊃BΓ⊢M≡λx.M(x):A⊃B(η⊃)\cfrac{\Gamma\vdash M:A\supset B}{\Gamma\vdash M\equiv\lambda x.M(x):A\supset B}(\eta\supset) Γ⊢M≡λx.M(x):A⊃BΓ⊢M:A⊃B​(η⊃)
